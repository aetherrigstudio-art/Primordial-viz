# Landing-page copy & voice

How to write the words on primordial's landing page so they're concise,
confident, and unmistakably the voice of an electronic-music visual artist — not
corporate filler or hype.

## The voice

Confident, concrete, a little understated. The work is intense and futuristic;
the writing doesn't need to shout to match it — restraint reads as someone who
knows what they're doing. Write like an artist talking about their craft, not a
SaaS landing page. Specifically:

- **Plain and direct** over buzzwords. Say what the thing *is* and *does* in
  language a musician or promoter understands. Avoid "leverage," "synergy,"
  "next-generation," "revolutionary," and other corporate tells — they signal the
  opposite of credibility.
- **Concrete over abstract.** "Room audio drives the visuals you operate live"
  beats "an immersive audio-reactive experience platform." Specifics are
  believable; abstractions are forgettable.
- **Confident, not boastful.** State capability as fact; let the visuals carry
  the awe. Overclaiming ("the most advanced visuals ever") undercuts trust;
  quiet certainty builds it.
- **A bit of edge.** The identity is grungy-future, so the voice can have texture
  — sharp, a little raw — as long as it stays clear. Personality is fine;
  confusion is not.

## Hero copy

The headline does the heaviest lifting on the page. It should name what this is
in one confident line that could only be about *this* — not a tagline so generic
it fits any brand. Test it against: "Could a stranger, in one read, tell what
this is and feel the vibe?" If it's vague poetry with no anchor, it fails.

The subline (one sentence) carries the "why" or "who" the headline can't — the
audience, the use, the feeling. Together: **headline = what + vibe; subline =
why it matters / who it's for.** Keep both short enough to read in a glance on a
phone.

Avoid the common hero mistakes: a headline that's an abstract mood with no
meaning, a paragraph where a line belongs, or jargon that assumes the visitor
already knows the product.

## CTA copy

The button is a verb the visitor *wants to do*, written from their point of view
— "See it live," "Start a collab," "Hear it react" — not a system label like
"Submit," "Learn more," or "Click here." Match the words to the one action the
page exists to produce, and use the same words everywhere that action appears
(`structure-and-conversion.md`). Specific, active, low-friction. If a secondary
action exists, phrase it so it clearly reads as secondary and never competes
with the primary verb.

## Body copy

Short. Landing-page body is not documentation — it's the minimum that makes a
visitor decide, with depth deferred (progressive disclosure). Favor:

- **Short sentences and short paragraphs.** Walls of text lose phone readers.
- **Specifics that prove the claim** — a real gig, a real collaborator, a real
  capability — over adjectives. "Played [venue]" beats "incredible live shows."
- **Active voice and present tense.** "It reacts to the room" not "the room can
  be reacted to."
- **One idea per block.** Each section says one thing clearly, then moves on.

## Microcopy

The small text — button hints, form labels, empty states, the mic-permission
prompt, error messages — is where craft shows. Make it human and clear: tell the
visitor what will happen and why ("We use your mic only to drive the visuals —
nothing is recorded or sent"), reassure on anything that feels risky (granting
mic), and keep error messages helpful rather than blaming. Good microcopy
quietly removes hesitation at exactly the moments a visitor might bail.

## What to avoid

- Corporate buzzwords and hype adjectives (covered above) — they erode trust.
- Vague mood-poetry headlines with no anchor to what this actually is.
- Walls of text; multi-paragraph heroes.
- CTA labels written about the system instead of the visitor's intent.
- Overclaiming or manufactured urgency — both read as inauthentic, which is fatal
  for an *artist's* page where authenticity is the product.
